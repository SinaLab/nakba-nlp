The 1st International Workshop on Nakba Narratives as Language Resources (Nakba-NLP 2025)

Abstract

Nakba-NLP 2025 seeks to tackle the crucial task of preserving and making accessible the historical, cultural, literary, and academic significance of Nakba narratives and testimonies. These valuable narratives exist in multiple formats, including manuscripts, books, audio recordings, novels, and films, necessitating their conversion into machine-readable formats for future preservation and usability. This workshop will delve into the utilisation of artificial intelligence, natural language processing, and corpus linguistics to achieve this goal. We will examine various techniques and tools that aid in creating accessible, comprehensive, and well-annotated digital collections. This initiative aims to enable researchers and historians to validate and disseminate critical insights from Nakba narratives. While the primary focus will be on datasets and narratives in Arabic, English, and other languages, all submitted papers should be in English.


Workshop Description

Nakba narratives and testimonies possess significant historical, cultural, literary, and academic value. Preserving these documents is crucial for ensuring their accessibility and usability for future generations. These narratives exist in diverse formats such as manuscripts, books, audio recordings, novels, and films. Converting this content into a machine-understandable format presents a notable challenge. Establishing accessible archives and well-annotated collections is essential for researchers and historians to verify and share meaningful information. This workshop aims to explore how artificial intelligence, natural language processing, and corpus linguistics can assist in preserving, understanding, and disseminating Nakba narratives and testimonies.

The workshop will investigate techniques and tools from natural language processing and corpus linguistics to address the challenges of transforming Nakba narratives into a machine-processable digital format. The goal is to create accessible, comprehensive, and well-annotated collections that empower researchers and historians to validate and share critical insights derived from these data. The workshop targets datasets and narratives in Arabic, English, and other languages, however, submitted articles should be written in English.


Motivation

The Nakba represents a pivotal event in Middle Eastern history, with its narratives and testimonies offering invaluable insights into the experiences and perspectives of those affected. These accounts are not only essential for historical documentation but also for understanding the cultural and literary heritage of the region. However, the preservation of these narratives is fraught with challenges due to their diverse formats and the risk of deterioration over time.

Advancements in artificial intelligence, natural language processing, and corpus linguistics present new opportunities to overcome these challenges. By converting Nakba narratives into machine-readable formats, we can ensure their preservation and facilitate broader accessibility and usability. This transformation will enable researchers and historians to systematically analyse these narratives, uncovering new insights and fostering a deeper understanding of the Nakba's impact.

The Nakba-NLP 2025 workshop is motivated by the urgent need to safeguard these narratives for future generations and to create a robust digital repository that supports academic and historical research. By bringing together experts in AI, NLP, and corpus linguistics, we aim to develop innovative solutions that address the complexities of this task and contribute to the preservation and dissemination of Nakba narratives in a meaningful and impactful way.

Call for Papers

We invite submissions for Nakba-NLP 2025, a workshop dedicated to the exploration and preservation of Nakba narratives through the application of artificial intelligence, natural language processing, and corpus linguistics. This workshop aims to develop valuable NLP resources by transforming these narratives into machine-readable formats, thus ensuring their accessibility and usability for future generations.

We emphasise that this workshop is not intended to focuses on the creation of NLP resources derived from Nakba narratives. All submitted papers must clearly state and explain their relevance to the topic of ‘Nakba Narratives as Language Resources’.   • We seek contributions on the following issues of interest
  • Digitisation of oral and written narratives
  • Creation and labeling of language corpora and datasets
  • Digital archives, metadata, and semantic/content mark-up
  • Annotation tools and annotation guidelines
  • Document classification, topic modeling, and information retrieval
  • Named entity recognition for identifying people, places, organizations, and events
  • Entity linking and relationship extraction
  • Event detection and event argument extraction
  • Knowledge Graphs and Linked Data
  • Vocabularies, dictionaries, and ontologies
  • Data visualisation
  • Knowledge representation
  • Machine translation, summarisation, and paraphrasing
  • Natural Language Generation
  • Large language models
  • Sentiment analysis and emotional content extraction
  • Discourse analysis (e.g., bias, offensive language, and misinformation) related to Nakba narratives

Participants are invited to use the following archives: Institute for Palestine Studies (Website), The Palestinian Museum (Website), Nakba-Archive (Website), POHA (Website), Alhaq (Website), ICHR (Website), as well as Wikipedia and the Wikidata Knowledge Graph.


Submission Details

All submitted papers must clearly state and explain their relevance to the topic of ‘Nakba Narratives as Language Resources’. The organisers reserve the right to reject any papers that incite hatred, refute established facts, or undermine the suffering of individuals.

Submissions may be of two types:

  • Long papers – up to eight (8) pages maximum, presenting substantial, original, completed, and unpublished work.
  • Short papers – up to four (4) pages, describing a small focused contribution, negative results, system demonstrations, etc.
